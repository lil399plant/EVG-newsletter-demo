Cover Feature · Software & AI

SaaSpocalypse 2026: Is AI Eating Software?

The S&P Software and Services index (SPSISS) dropped 27% last quarter — the worst crash since the Global Financial Crisis. The index fell to roughly 20× forward earnings, down from 25–30×+ during the cloud boom. The market is repricing a structural question: what happens to legacy SaaS when AI can generate code, automate workflows, and undercut per-seat pricing models?

"CIOs are shifting budget away from SaaS seats toward AI infrastructure. The marginal cost of new software is approaching zero."

The answer, we argue, lies in a bifurcation. AI-enabled software names face margin pressure because large-scale AI runs at roughly 50% gross margins — far below traditional SaaS at 70–80%. There is a fundamental pricing mismatch: vendors pay usage-based AI opex to hyperscalers (Microsoft Azure, AWS, GCP) but still charge per-seat SaaS pricing. This model breaks under scale.

The Bifurcation Thesis

Not all SaaS is created equal in the age of LLMs. Mission-critical platforms with deep workflow integration — think ServiceNow, Salesforce CRM data layers, or ERP systems — can survive, even thrive, by selling high-value, AI-integrated full-stack solutions at premium pricing. Their switching costs remain moat-like.

Narrow, vertically siloed point solutions are a different story. If an LLM can replicate 80% of the functionality of a single-purpose SaaS tool in a custom prompt chain, the addressable market for that tool collapses. This is already visible in categories like transcription, basic document summarization, and code review copilots.

Single-name selection or custom long/short baskets screening for AI integration depth, switching costs, and gross margin trajectory.

Markets Update · Equities

Q4 2025 Earnings Season: Soft Landing Still Intact

Roughly 60% of S&P 500 constituents have now reported. Blended EPS growth is tracking high-single to low-teens percent year-over-year — potentially the fifth consecutive quarter of double-digit earnings growth. Approximately 75% of reporters beat EPS expectations; revenue surprises are also running above long-run averages.

The "soft landing" narrative continues to be underwritten by earnings fundamentals. The economy is being powered by three engines: AI-driven capex, cloud and digital infrastructure build-out, and higher-income services demand that has proved resistant to rate headwinds.

Alphabet posted a strong Q4, with 2025 capex near the mid-$90 billion area and even larger AI infrastructure spend guided for 2026. This is both a signal of confidence and a warning for free cash flow bulls.

Markets Update · Rates

Yields Gyrate on Payrolls and CPI

U.S. Treasury yields ended the week slightly lower after a highly volatile path. Two delayed BLS data releases drove the intraweek action: the jobs report (released Wednesday) and CPI data (Friday), both delayed from their originally scheduled dates.

Midweek, stronger-than-expected payroll growth with unemployment holding at approximately 4.3% caused traders to fear the Fed would have less urgency to cut rates, pushing yields meaningfully higher. By week's end, a softer-than-expected CPI reading reversed the move, bringing yields back below where they started.

"The yield curve is being pulled in opposite directions: stronger labor market data argues for higher-for-longer, while softening inflation supports the dovish pivot trade."

FOMC Outlook

The FOMC dot plot continues to imply two to three cuts in 2026, but the path has become increasingly data-dependent. Market pricing via fed funds futures has oscillated between one and three cuts this year, reflecting the binary nature of the macro data flow. The next meaningful catalyst is the March meeting, where the committee will update its Summary of Economic Projections.

The softer CPI print provided relief to duration buyers, but the underlying picture remains complex. Services inflation, driven by shelter and insurance costs, remains elevated. Goods deflation — partly the result of import price pressure — is providing an offset. The net result is a Fed that wants to cut but needs further confirmation.

FX Story of the Week: Dollar-Yen

USD/JPY slid nearly 3% — the week's defining move in global FX markets. The catalyst was the election victory of Sanae Takaichi, which prompted a sharp short-covering rally in the yen as currency speculators who were net-short JPY rushed to cover positions.

The macro context matters beyond the immediate trigger. A sustained yen strengthening has meaningful downstream effects on global markets. If Bank of Japan policy normalization proceeds — pushing Japanese government bond yields higher — Japanese investors face reduced incentive to channel savings into U.S. Treasuries in search of yield. This capital staying in Japan rather than recycling into U.S. duration would push U.S. Treasury yields higher without any action from the Federal Reserve.

"Higher U.S. Treasury yields driven by Japanese capital repatriation would mean more expensive mortgages, tighter corporate borrowing conditions, and added complexity to the Fed's inflation outlook."

Crypto Corner · Stablecoins

Stablecoins: The Room-Temperature Superconductor of Finance

A stablecoin is a tokenized dollar (or euro) issued on a blockchain, backed by cash and short-term government bills. Think of it as fiat economics running on crypto settlement rails — a room-temperature superconductor combining the technological agility of crypto with the monetary certainty of central-bank-backed fiat.

"Stablecoins do for money what email did for letters — near-instant, near-zero-cost transmission of value, with programmability that SWIFT cannot replicate."

Key Use Cases

Cross-border payments. Stablecoins replace slow, expensive SWIFT/correspondent banking with near-instant, near-zero-cost settlement. Programmable features enable automatic payroll, subscriptions, and conditional payment release — all without a clearing bank in the middle.

Institutional settlement. JPM Coin and euro-denominated deposit tokens are already live. Firms settle trades in minutes rather than T+2, releasing working capital and reducing counterparty exposure. The operational efficiency gains are material at institutional scale.

Micropayments. Card networks are too expensive for pay-per-article, per-API call, or per-GPU-minute billing. Stablecoins enable sub-cent transactions that would be uneconomic via traditional rails — a critical infrastructure layer for AI-native business models.

Financial inclusion. People in weak-currency jurisdictions with capital controls can hold dollar stablecoins as a synthetic way to access the purchasing power of short-term U.S. Treasuries via any smartphone.

Technicals Corner · Interview Prep

This Week's Questions

Investment Banking
When a company borrows $100 of debt to issue stock, how do enterprise value and equity value change?
Show Answer ▾

Enterprise Value (EV) remains unchanged. EV = Equity Value + Net Debt. When a company raises $100 of debt, net debt increases by $100. Simultaneously, equity value decreases by $100 (the cash used for the buyback leaves the business, reducing its intrinsic value to equity holders). The two effects offset: EV is constant.

Equity Value decreases in total, but equity value per share may increase if the shares are retired at a price below intrinsic value. This is the core thesis behind accretive buybacks.

Key caveat: If the debt is used for a buyback above intrinsic value, equity value per share is destroyed. The accounting mechanics are neutral, but the economics depend entirely on the price paid.

Sales & Trading
What is the difference between Macaulay duration and modified duration?
Show Answer ▾

Macaulay Duration is the weighted average time to receive the bond's cash flows, measured in years. Each cash flow is weighted by its present value as a proportion of the bond's total price. It is a measure of time, not price sensitivity.

Modified Duration converts Macaulay Duration into a price sensitivity measure: it estimates the percentage change in a bond's price for a 1% (100 bps) change in yield. The formula is: Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration ÷ (1 + y/n), where y is the yield and n is the number of compounding periods per year.

Practical use: Traders use modified duration to hedge interest rate risk. A bond with modified duration of 5 will lose approximately 5% in price for every 1% rise in rates (all else equal). For large yield moves, convexity must also be incorporated.

Brain Teaser
How many soccer balls can fit into Morgan Stanley's headquarters?
Show Answer ▾

This is a Fermi estimation problem. The approach matters as much as the answer.

Step 1 — Estimate the building volume. 1585 Broadway is approximately 50 floors with ~25,000 sq ft per floor. Ceiling height ~3m. Total volume ≈ 50 × 25,000 × 3 = 3.75M cubic feet ≈ 106,000 m³.

Step 2 — Usable space. Apply a 70% packing efficiency factor (random close packing of spheres is ~64%; accounting for walls and columns, use 60–65%). Usable ≈ 0.65 × 106,000 m³ ≈ 69,000 m³.

Step 3 — Soccer ball volume. Diameter ~22cm → radius 0.11m → volume = (4/3)π(0.11)³ ≈ 0.00559 m³.

Result: 69,000 ÷ 0.00559 ≈ 12.3 million soccer balls. Reasonable range: 10–15 million.
